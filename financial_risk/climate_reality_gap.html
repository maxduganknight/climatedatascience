Climate Correction Events

Climate Correction 1 (2032)

Rapidly accelerating physical risk is outpacing insurance markets and governments' ability to adapt. Extreme events on the scale of the 2025 LA wildfires and Hurricane Katrina will eventually occur simultaneously. Private and subsidized insurance markets will fail, billions of dollars of real estate value will be wiped out, and the shockwaves will ripple through the financial markets a la 2008. Except in this case, the underlying cause will not be financial, it will be physical, and it will only get worse.

Climate Correction 2 (2045)

It may well take more than one correction for markets to calibrate to new models of value on a less stable planet. At this point, the climate crisis will crash into the political realm as crop failure spreads, food security disappears for millions, emigration from the hardest hit areas soars, extreme weather disrupts global trade, and governments resort to conflict over scarce resources. Persistent global conflict at this stage would mean markets do not recover at all and the picture would look even bleaker than pictured.

Why The Gap Exists: The Climate Risk Dilution Pipeline

Physical climate reality gets progressively filtered and diluted through multiple institutional barriers, explaining why market valuations are so far from climate-adjusted reality.

1

Physical Climate Reality

100%

The complete scope of climate risk includes tipping points, cascading failures, extreme weather events, infrastructure damage, supply chain disruptions, resource scarcity, and systemic economic breakdowns. This represents the actual physical reality of what climate change means for economic systems.

At this stage, all risk factors that could impact economic value are present: ice sheet collapse, Amazon dieback, permafrost methane release, crop failures, mass migration, infrastructure destruction, and the compounding effects when multiple systems fail simultaneously.

⬇
2

Climate Science Filter

75%

Scientific conservatism systematically excludes high-risk, low-probability events from climate models. As Simon Sharpe explains in "Five Times Faster," scientists don't publish findings until proven beyond reasonable doubt. Proving highly uncertain outcomes like tipping points is nearly impossible, so these risks don't make it into most climate models.

There's enormous pressure not to overstate climate risk. Fossil fuel interests attack any finding that appears to exaggerate, making conservatism a survival trait for career success in climate science. The result: published climate science systematically underestimates risk by excluding the most dangerous but uncertain scenarios.

Filtered out: Tipping point cascades, worst-case scenarios, deep uncertainty
⬇
3

Climate Economics Translation

50%

Economics and climate science don't speak the same language. This creates a fundamental translation problem where the latest economic papers are (a) out of date with climate research, and (b) consistently underestimate climate's economic impact.

Economists approach climate risk assuming a baseline economy that grows at consistent rates minus climate's negative impacts. But this misses the fundamental truth: economic growth depends on the stable climate it has relied on until now. The climate isn't some small external variable affecting the economy—the economy exists within and because of the climate.

Economics hasn't developed compelling models for the climate-economy relationship. Most economic premises about how climate risk works are fundamentally flawed, treating climate as an external shock rather than the foundation that enables economic activity.

Filtered out: Climate-economy interdependence, system-level breakdowns, non-linear impacts
⬇
4

Consulting & Research Commercialization

30%

A significant gap exists between cutting-edge academic research and how private markets assess climate risk. Research takes years to percolate into commercial climate modeling used by financial institutions.

Consulting companies have perverse incentives around model updates. What's the business incentive to update models faster? Updates only mean delivering worse news to clients who are paying for the analysis. Commercial pressures favor models that don't disrupt client planning or create uncomfortable conversations about fundamental business model risks.

The result is systematically outdated risk models that lag academic research by years, miss recent climate developments, and tend toward conservative assumptions that won't alarm clients or trigger difficult strategic questions.

Filtered out: Latest research findings, model updates that worsen outlook, uncomfortable strategic implications
⬇
5

Financial Market Incentives

15%

Even when financial institutions receive realistic climate risk modeling, they have little incentive to publicize or act on cautionary models because the stock market cares about short-term value.

Multiple institutional pressures work against incorporating long-term climate risks: quarterly earnings pressure, career incentives tied to short-term performance, competitive disadvantages from being "first mover" on climate concerns, and investor expectations for continuous growth regardless of long-term sustainability.

The fundamental structure of financial markets—quarterly reporting, annual bonuses, multi-year investment cycles—systematically filters out risks that manifest over decades. Climate risk falls into this blind spot almost perfectly.

Filtered out: Long-term risks, scenarios that threaten growth assumptions, competitive disadvantages from climate honesty
⬇
6

Final Asset Valuations

5%

The end result: asset valuations that incorporate only a tiny fraction of actual climate risk. This massive gap between physical reality and market pricing creates the climate reality gap visible in the chart above.

We can see this dilution everywhere: minimal climate attention in financial reporting, growth assumptions in 2050+ financial models that ignore climate uncertainty, and systematic "language drift" where climate scientists' "existential threats" become "growth headwinds" in financial reports.

Each institutional filter has removed layers of risk, creating a financial system that prices assets as if climate change were a minor external factor rather than a fundamental threat to the economic foundations those assets depend on.
